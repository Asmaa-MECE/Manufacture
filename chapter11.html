1. What is Joining?

Joining is an all-inclusive term covering processes such as welding, brazing, soldering, adhesive bonding, and mechanical fastening. These processes permanently or semi-permanently connect two or more parts at their contacting surfaces.

Why Do We Need Joining?

  • Some products are impossible to manufacture as a single piece — joining is the only option (e.g., a thin-walled tubular assembly with long arms)
  • Some products are easier and more economical to make in separate components that are then joined
  • Products need to be disassembled for maintenance or inspection, so mechanical fastening is preferred
  • Different materials are required in the same product for different functional zones (e.g., carbide cutting insert brazed to a steel drill shank)
  • Manufacturing in components is easier for transportation and packaging

Examples in a Typical Automobile

  • Spot-welded car body panels
  • Brazed joint for emission control components
  • Soldered electrical circuitry
  • Bolted engine assembly and mechanical fastening of body trim
  • Adhesively bonded windshield to car body
  • Welded exhaust pipes; seam-welded muffler

2. Types of Joints

The five fundamental joint geometries used across all joining processes:

Joint TypeDescription
Butt jointParts lie in the same plane and are joined at their edges. Simple, economical, limited by thickness — thicker joints need edge preparation (V-groove, etc.)
Corner jointParts form a right angle and are joined at the corner of that angle
Tee joint (T-joint)One part is perpendicular to the other, forming a T shape
Lap jointTwo overlapping parts joined at their overlapping area
Edge jointAt least one edge between the two pieces is common / shared

Weld Groove Types for Butt Joints

  • Square groove — for thin sections; no edge preparation needed
  • Single V-groove — both parts bevelled; suitable for medium thickness
  • Double V-groove — welding from both sides; requires less filler metal than single V for the same thickness
  • Bevel, U, J grooves — various geometries for thicker sections and specific applications

⚠️ Key exam point: A double V-groove weld requires less filler metal than a single V-groove weld of the same thickness, because material is removed from both sides instead of only one.

3. Welding — Overview

Welding is a material joining process in which two or more parts are coalesced at their contacting surfaces by a suitable application of heat and/or pressure. In some processes, a filler material is added to facilitate coalescence. Welding is most commonly associated with metals but is also used for plastics.

Advantages of Welding

  • Provides a permanent joint
  • With a proper filler metal, the welded joint can be stronger than the parent materials
  • Economical in terms of material usage and fabrication costs

Disadvantages of Welding

  • Most welding is manual → high labor costs
  • Can be dangerous (heat, pressure, radiation, fumes)
  • Disassembly is not possible — the joint is permanent
  • Some weld defects are difficult to detect, compromising structural integrity

Two Major Categories of Welding

Fusion Welding

  • Material is melted and coalesced using heat (chemical or electrical)
  • Filler metals may or may not be used
  • Examples: oxyfuel-gas, arc welding (SMAW, GMAW, TIG), resistance, electron beam, laser beam

Solid-State Welding

  • Joining occurs without fusion — no liquid (molten) phase in the joint
  • Uses pressure, diffusion, or mechanical energy
  • Examples: cold welding, friction welding, ultrasonic welding, diffusion bonding, explosion welding

4. Fusion Welding Processes

Chemical — Oxyfuel-Gas Welding (OFW)

  • Various fuels (most commonly acetylene) are mixed with oxygen and burned through a torch
  • Acetylene is favored because it generates temperatures up to 3,500 °C
  • A filler rod coated with flux is sometimes used to prevent oxidation and create a better joint
  • The flame comes from the torch; the molten pool of base metal + filler solidifies to form the weld

Electric — Arc Welding (General)

  • Heat is generated by an electric arc between the electrode and the workpiece
  • The arc is initiated when the electrode contacts the work and is then quickly separated to maintain the gap
  • Arc temperature reaches approximately 5,500 °C — sufficient to melt most metals
  • Molten metal (base + filler) solidifies in the weld region

Shielded Metal Arc Welding (SMAW) — "Stick Welding"

  • Uses a consumable electrode — a filler metal rod coated with flux and shielding gas chemicals
  • The coating burns to produce shielding gas around the weld pool and a slag layer on top
  • Filler rod coating: powdered cellulose mixed with carbonates, bound with silicate
  • Chemical composition of filler metal is usually very close to the base metal
  • The electrode is consumed and must be changed periodically → reduces production rate (cannot be automated as easily)
  • Slag must be removed by brushing after each pass

Gas Metal Arc Welding (GMAW) — "MIG Welding"

  • Another consumable electrode process — but the wire is continuous from a reel
  • Shielding gas (helium or argon for MIG; CO₂ for steel welding) is supplied separately via a nozzle around the arc
  • No slag — can be used for multiple weld passes without inter-pass cleaning
  • The continuous electrode means the process can be fully automated — much higher production rate than SMAW
  • Also called Metal Inert Gas (MIG) welding when using argon or helium

Submerged Arc Welding (SAW)

  • Uses a continuous bare electrode wire; shielding is provided by granular flux from a hopper that completely submerges the arc
  • Because the arc is submerged, there are no sparks, spatter, or radiation → no safety glasses or gloves required
  • Slow cooling rate due to the flux blanket → excellent weld quality
  • Unfused flux is collected and can be reused

Gas Tungsten Arc Welding (GTAW) — "TIG Welding"

  • Uses a non-consumable tungsten electrode with an inert shielding gas (argon or helium)
  • Also called Tungsten Inert Gas (TIG) welding
  • Filler wire is optional — when used, it is fed separately into the weld pool (heated by arc, not transferred across it)
  • Tungsten is ideal because its melting point is ~3,400 °C
  • Advantages: highest quality welds, no spatter, minimal post-weld cleaning (no flux used)

5. Electrodes, Shielding & Flux

Consumable vs Non-Consumable Electrodes

TypeDescriptionNotes
ConsumableRod or wire (200–450 mm length, <10 mm diameter) that provides the filler metal source; consumed by the arc and added to the weld poolMust be replaced periodically (SMAW) or fed continuously (GMAW); reduces production rate in SMAW
Non-consumableDoes not melt into the weld; some depletion occurs by vaporization; filler metal supplied separately if neededUsed in TIG; allows finer control of heat and filler independently

Shielding Gas

  • The weld area (arc, electrode tip, weld pool) is shielded from the atmosphere by a blanket of gas
  • Objective: prevent exposure to oxygen, nitrogen, and hydrogen present in air (causes porosity, embrittlement, oxidation)
  • Common shielding gases: Argon and Helium (inert); CO₂ for steel (active)

Flux

  • Protects the weld region from formation of oxides and other contaminants, or dissolves them for easy removal
  • During welding, flux melts and covers the weld region → solidifies into slag → must be removed by brushing
  • Flux also stabilizes the arc and reduces spatter

6. Weld Morphology & Grain Structure

A typical fusion weld has four distinct zones:

ZoneDescription
Fusion Zone (Weld Metal)A mixture of filler and base metal that have completely melted and resolidified. Grains are coarse, columnar, and grow perpendicular to the weld interface (solidification direction).
Weld InterfaceA narrow boundary separating the fusion zone from the heat-affected zone. A thin layer of base metal that was partially melted.
Heat-Affected Zone (HAZ)Between the fusion zone and unaffected base metal. Temperature was below melting point but high enough to alter microstructure and therefore mechanical properties.
Unaffected Base MetalThe parent material beyond the HAZ, unaffected by the welding heat.

🔑 Key fact: Grains in the solidified weld metal grow perpendicular to their interface with the base metal — they grow toward the heat source (outward from the fusion boundary). Deep welds have longer columnar grains than shallow welds.

The micro-hardness profile across a weld bead typically shows the highest hardness at the center of the fusion zone, decreasing through the HAZ toward the unaffected base metal.

Ferrous Materials

MaterialWeldability
Low-carbon steelExcellent
Medium-carbon steelFair to good
High-carbon steelPoor

High-alloy steelGood under well-controlled conditions
Stainless steelGenerally weldable
Cast ironGenerally weldable

Non-Ferrous Materials

  • Aluminium alloys — weldable with high heat input, inert gas shielding, and absence of moisture
  • Aluminium alloys containing Zinc or Copper — un-weldable
  • Magnesium alloys — weldable with shielding gas or flux
    • Titanium alloys — weldable with shielding gas

8. Weld Defects

Porosity

  • Caused by: gases released during melting and trapped during solidification; chemical reactions; contaminants
  • Remedy: proper electrode and filler metal selection; preheat the weld area; clean the weld zone; reduce welding speed to allow gas to escape

Slag Inclusions

  • Compounds (oxides, fluxes, electrode-coating materials) trapped in the weld zone
  • Remedy: clean the weld-bead surface before depositing the next layer; provide sufficient shielding gas; redesign the joint for better access

Cracks

  • Can be longitudinal, transverse, toe, crater, or underbead cracks
  • Caused by: temperature gradients causing thermal stresses; variations in weld composition causing differential contraction rates during cooling

Incomplete Fusion & Incomplete Penetration

  • Incomplete fusion (lack of fusion) — the weld metal does not fully bond with the base metal; bridging occurs leaving voids at the interface
  • Incomplete penetration — weld metal does not extend through the full joint thickness

Distortion

  • Caused by differential thermal expansion and contraction of different regions of the welded assembly
  • Types: transverse shrinkage, longitudinal shrinkage, angular distortion, bowing/buckling

Other Surface Defects

  • Undercut — groove melted into the base metal adjacent to the weld toe, not filled by weld metal
  • Overlap — weld metal flows over the base metal surface without fusing to it
  • Underfill — the weld cross-section is below the surface of the base metal

9. Brazing & Soldering

Both brazing and soldering join materials using a filler metal that melts at a temperature lower than the base metals. The base metals do not melt — only the filler metal does. The joint forms by capillary action drawing the liquid filler into the gap.

Key Distinction — Temperature

🔑 Brazing: Filler metal melts above 450 °C (below the base metal solidus). Produces stronger joints than soldering. Used for: carbide inserts on drill shanks, copper pipe fittings, heat exchangers.

Soldering: Filler metal (solder) melts below 450 °C. Much lower strength. Used for: electrical circuit connections, plumbing (soft solder).

Brazing vs Welding

AttributeBrazingWelding
Base metal meltingNo — base metal stays solidYes — base metal melts
Joint strengthGood, but weaker than base metalCan exceed base metal strength
Dissimilar materialsExcellent — can join very different materialsLimited by metallurgical compatibility
Distortion riskLower (lower temperatures)Higher
HAZMinimal or noneSignificant HAZ present

Classic Brazing Example

A carbide cutting insert brazed to a low-alloy steel drill body — the two materials (ceramic-like carbide and steel) cannot be fusion-welded due to metallurgical incompatibility, but brazing joins them effectively without melting either base material.
