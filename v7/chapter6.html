1. What is Metal Forming?

Metal forming involves shaping metal through mechanical deformation — no material is added or removed; the mass of the workpiece stays constant.

  • Plastic deformation changes shape; stresses exceed the yield stress of the material.
  • Stresses are usually compressive (rolling, forging, extrusion).
  • Some processes apply tensile stresses (stretching), combined tensile & compressive (bending), or shear stresses.

Effect of Temperature

  • Higher temperature → increased ductility + reduced yield stress.
  • Forming can result in lower yield stress and higher ductility — desirable properties.

Two Main Categories

  • Bulk Deformation – massive shape changes, low surface-to-volume ratio: Rolling, Forging, Extrusion, Wire Drawing.
  • Sheet Metalworking – Bending, Deep Drawing, Shearing.

2. Metal Rolling

Rolling reduces the thickness or changes the cross-section of a long workpiece using compressive forces from rotating rolls. It accounts for 90% of all metals produced by metalworking.

Products

  • Plates — thickness > 6 mm (e.g., boilers: 300 mm, reactor vessels: 150 mm, battleships: 100–125 mm).
  • Sheets — thickness < 6 mm (automobile bodies, beverage cans: 0.28 mm, aluminum foil: 0.008 mm).

Types by Geometry

  • Flat Rolling – reduces rectangular cross-section thickness.
  • Shape Rolling – converts square cross-section into I-beams, channels, rails.
  • Ring Rolling – seamless rings (bearing rings, turbine disks, gear blanks).
  • Thread Rolling – produces threads (cold forming; stronger than machined threads).
  • Skew Rolling – produces steel balls for ball bearings.

Flat Rolling Formulas

Draft: d = h₀ − h_f  |  Reduction ratio: r = d / h₀
Max draft: d_max = μ²R (R = roll radius)
Roll force: F = LwY_avg where L = √(R·d)
Power (kW): P = 2πNFL / 60,000

Reducing Roll Force

  • Reduce friction | Use smaller-diameter rolls | Take smaller reductions per pass
  • Roll at elevated temperature | Apply longitudinal tension to the strip

Rolling Defects

  • Wavy edges – caused by roll bending.
  • Cracks (zipper/edge) – poor material ductility at rolling temperature.
  • Alligatoring – nonuniform deformation or original cast defects.

3. Forging

One of the oldest metalworking operations (8,000 B.C.), forging shapes a workpiece using compressive forces through dies and tools. Forged parts have excellent strength and toughness due to favorable grain flow.

Types by Temperature

  • Cold Forging – greater forces required; better surface finish & dimensional accuracy.
  • Hot Forging – smaller forces; less accuracy and poorer surface finish.

Types by Die Configuration

  • Open-Die Forging (Upsetting) – between two flat dies; metal flows laterally. Used for large parts (up to 275 tons, shafts up to 23 m).
  • Closed-Die Forging (Impression-Die) – die contains cavity; flash is created. Stages: Edging → Blocking → Finishing → Trimming.
  • Flashless Forging – metal completely constrained, no flash.

Open-Die Forging Force Formula

F = Y_f · A · (1 + μD/3h)
Flow stress: Y_f = Kε^n  |  True strain: ε = ln(h₀/h)

Other Forging Operations

  • Cogging – successive open-die steps to reduce bar thickness.
  • Coining – closed-die for coins, medallions, jewelry.
  • Swaging – rotary process to reduce cross-section of rods/tubes.

Forging Defects

  • Insufficient material – web may buckle and develop laps.
  • Too much material – excess flows past forged portions, internal cracks form.

Die Design Rules

  • Material flows in direction of least resistance.
  • Parting line is usually at the largest cross-section.
  • Draft angles are mandatory for die removal.

4. Extrusion

Material is forced through a die to produce a constant cross-section product — like squeezing toothpaste. Typical products: door/window frames, railings, tubing, structural shapes.

Types

  • Direct (Forward) – billet pushed through stationary die; friction between billet & container wall.
  • Indirect (Reverse/Backward) – die moves toward billet; less friction, less force. Supporting product on exit is difficult.
  • Hydrostatic – fluid fills container; no friction with container walls; cold extrusion only.
  • Impact – punch descends rapidly; billet extruded backward (cold forming).

Extrusion Force Formula

F = A₀ · k · ln(A₀/A_f)
Extrusion ratio = A₀/A_f (typically 10:1 to 100:1, up to 400:1)

Advantages

  • Wide variety of shapes | Enhanced grain structure & strength | Little material waste | Close tolerances (cold extrusion)

Disadvantage

  • Cross-section must be uniform throughout the length of the part.

5. Wire Drawing

The cross-section of a rod or wire is reduced or changed by pulling it through a die (unlike extrusion which pushes). Key variables: cross-sectional area reduction, die angle, friction, and speed.

  • Maximum reduction in area per pass: 63%.
  • Very long products (km of wire) are drawn using multi-stage rotating drum machines.
  • Common cross-sections: less than 13 mm diameter.
