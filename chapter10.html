1. What is Additive Manufacturing?

Additive Manufacturing (AM) refers to a process by which digital 3D design data is used to build up a component in layers by depositing material. The term '3D printing' is increasingly used as a synonym, but AM is more precise — it describes a professional production technique clearly distinguished from conventional material removal.

🔑 Core distinction: Machining is top-down (material removal). AM is bottom-up (material addition). No tooling is needed in AM — the digital file drives the process directly.

3D printing was first invented at MIT in the mid-1980s and patented in 1995. It involves building parts layer-by-layer from powder-based or filament material.

Why is AM Such a Big Deal Now?

  • Wide availability of CAD/CAM software
  • Improved automation and component technologies
  • A growing library of printable materials
  • Major industry (GE, Airbus) and government investment (UK, USA, Germany, China)
  • Freedom to operate enabled by patent expirations
  • Momentum, confidence, and creative vision

Why Use AM?

  • Fast / rapid prototyping — physical part from a CAD file in hours
  • Complex geometries — internal channels, lattices, undercuts impossible to machine
  • Multiple and new materials — including mixed/graded materials
  • Enhanced performance — usually through weight reduction (topology optimisation)
  • Low-volume personalised / customised manufacturing — cost-neutral regardless of batch size

How AM Redefines Value

In traditional manufacturing, cost per unit (C/N) = F/N + V, meaning high fixed cost F dominates at low volume. AM largely eliminates fixed tooling cost — investing in a 3D printer (a broad capability) allows different parts to be produced on demand in any quantity (1, 10, or 100) without a separate fixed investment per design. This flattens the cost-vs-complexity curve dramatically.

How Are AM Parts Used? (Wohlers Report 2017)

  • Functional parts — 33.8% (largest category)
  • Fit and assembly — 16.0%
  • Education/research — 10.7%
  • Patterns for metal castings — 8.3%
  • Tooling components — 7.4%
  • Patterns for prototype tooling — 7.4%
  • Visual aids — 7.3%
  • Presentation models — 7.1%

3. Fused Deposition Modelling (FDM / FFF)

FDM (Fused Deposition Modelling) — also called FFF (Fused Filament Fabrication) — is the most common AM process, used in the majority of desktop 3D printers.

How It Works

  • A thermoplastic filament is fed through a heated build head (extruder) that moves in the x–y plane
  • The table (build platform) moves downward in the z-direction after each layer
  • Successive layers of melted plastic are deposited and fuse together to build the part from the bottom up
  • A plastic model can be created in minutes

Fill Patterns

Each layer is built in three stages: a perimeter trajectory outlines the boundary, then an in-fill trajectory fills the interior. Fill density can be varied (sparse/honeycomb interior saves material and time).

The Extruder

  • Often FDM machines have a dual extruder — one for the build material, one for the support material
  • A pinch wheel (drive wheel) grips the filament and provides force to counter the viscous resistance in the nozzle
  • At high feed rates, the filament can fail by shear under the drive wheel — this limits maximum rate and quality
  • Force increases with feed rate and is greater at lower temperature

FDM — Support Structures, Anisotropy, and Surface Finish

  • Support structures are required for overhanging features; they are removed post-build
  • Anisotropy — FDM parts are stronger in the x–y (in-plane) direction than in z (build direction); fracture preferentially occurs between layers
  • Surface finishing — the staircase (layer) effect requires post-processing (sanding, acetone vapour for ABS) for smooth surfaces

Industrial Applications

Every Boeing and Airbus aircraft has hundreds of parts made using FDM. GE, Airbus, and military programmes all rely on FDM for ducting, brackets, and housings.

✔ Advantages of FDM

  • Most common and accessible AM process
  • Low-cost feedstock (filament spools)
  • Wide variety of thermoplastics (ABS, PLA, PETG, Nylon, PEEK)
  • Simple operating conditions
  • Dual material capability (build + support)

✗ Limitations of FDM

  • Lowest resolution and surface quality of the three main AM processes
  • Anisotropic mechanical properties (weak in z-direction)
  • Support structures needed for overhangs
  • Staircase surface finish requires post-processing

4. Stereolithography (SLA)

Stereolithography was the first AM process to be commercialised. It cures a liquid photo-resin using a UV laser — a process called light-activated polymerisation.

How SLA Works

  • A UV laser beam is scanned across the surface of a vat of UV-curable liquid resin
  • Where the laser strikes, the resin cures (solidifies) to form each point and layer
  • The build platform moves down by one layer thickness after each layer is cured
  • SLA is a single-material process — only one resin per build

SLA vs FDM — Key Differences

AttributeSLAFDM

Surface qualityHigher — smoother finishLower — visible layer lines
Build speedSlower (finer layers)Faster (thicker layers)

Applications of SLA

  • High-detail presentation models for architects and automotive designers
  • Parts for customer visualisation and design reviews
  • Hearing aids — one of the largest-volume SLA production applications; fully personalised shell
  • Orthodontic braces / clear aligners — batch-personalised SLA masters used to thermoform aligner sheets

5. Selective Laser Sintering / Melting (SLS / SLM)

SLS and SLM use a high-power laser to locally sinter (SLS — partial fusion, used for plastics) or fully melt (SLM — used for metals) powder particles in a bed, building the part layer by layer.

How SLS / SLM Works

  • A thin layer of powder is spread over the build platform by a recoater blade
  • A laser scans the cross-section of the part, fusing the powder
  • The platform drops by one layer thickness; fresh powder is spread; process repeats
  • The unfused surrounding powder acts as a self-supporting medium — no separate support structures needed (a key advantage over FDM and SLA for metals)

Critical Process Parameters in SLM

  • Laser power
  • Laser scan speed
  • Laser scan pattern (hatch strategy)
  • Particle size and packing density (typically 10–100 µm diameter)
  • Layer uniformity and thickness
  • Bed (preheat) temperature

Mechanical Properties of SLM Parts

  • Fine microstructure → high strength (higher UTS than wrought reference material in some cases)
  • Small internal defects (porosity, lack-of-fusion) → lower ductility than standard wrought material
  • Properties highly dependent on process parameters and post-process annealing / HIP

SLM Powder Handling Challenges

  • Powder can be flammable (especially aluminium and titanium)
  • Inhalation risk — requires specialist handling and PPE
  • Metals oxidise easily — inert atmosphere (argon or nitrogen) required
  • Post-processing needed: powder removal, machining away metal supports, often HIP (Hot Isostatic Pressing)

Applications of SLM

  • Aerospace — GE LEAP fuel nozzle (was 20 parts, now 1 printed part; 25% lighter, 5× more durable)
  • Automotive — weight-optimised brackets and topology-optimised structural components
  • Tooling for injection moulding — conformal cooling channels impossible to drill conventionally
  • Medical implants — personalised titanium orthopaedic and craniofacial implants
  • Titanium bicycle frames — 3D printed mountain bike: original 2,100 g → redesigned 1,400 g (33% weight saving)

6. Material and Binder Jetting

Jetting processes use inkjet technology to deposit material locally. They are attractive because inkjet is a mature, parallelisable technology capable of depositing many droplets in a very short time.

  • Material Jetting — the part is built directly by jetting polymer droplets (usually UV-curable); produces multi-material, multi-colour parts with high accuracy
  • Binder Jetting — a liquid binder is deposited to fuse plastic, metal, or ceramic powder together; the green part is then sintered in a furnace

7. Emerging AM Technologies

  • Industrial Automation — creating automated production lines for 3D printing (e.g. MetalFAB1 by Additive Industries), removing human bottlenecks in powder handling, build, and post-processing
  • Hybrid AM + Machining — combined machines (especially Japanese-made) deposit metal via AM then machine it to net-shape in the same setup; very high cost ($1–2 million) but produce finished parts with no secondary operations
  • Large-scale FDM — organisations including ORNL have printed car bodies and military vehicle components using very large FDM systems
  • Multi-material and embedded electronics — printing conductive traces directly into parts

The Verdict — Where AM Wins

🏆 For large-scale manufacturing, AM cannot compete with conventional processes on cost, rate, or quality. But for flexibility — especially complex geometries, custom parts, and low volumes — the opportunities are immense. Complexity and customisation are effectively FREE in AM, unlike in conventional tooling-based processes.

Challenges to Accelerate AM Adoption

  • Design tools and data management to fully realise geometric freedom
  • Process control — higher quality at faster rates; part qualification standards
  • Standards (materials characterisation, process certification)
  • Education — from fundamentals to industrial practice
  • AM has potential to modify digital supply chains, shifting from global to local manufacturing
