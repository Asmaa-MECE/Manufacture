1. What is Thermoforming?

Thermoforming is the process of forming a thermoplastic sheet by applying heat to soften it, then pressure (vacuum, mechanical, or air) to press it against a mold until it takes the mold's shape and cools solid. Common products include plastic cups, fridge bodies, and fruit containers.

🔑 Core idea: A flat thermoplastic sheet is heated until rubbery, then stretched and formed against a mold using vacuum or pressure. It is fundamentally a sheet stretching process — unlike injection molding which fills a cavity with melt.

2. The Thermoforming Process & Machine

In thermoforming, a continuous roll of thermoplastic sheet feeds through three sequential zones in the machine: the heating zone, the forming zone, and the cooling/trimming zone.

Three Rate-Limiting Steps

The production rate of thermoforming is governed by whichever of these three steps is slowest:

  • 1. Heating — radiative heat transfer from infrared lamps to the plastic sheet (most commonly the bottleneck in continuous thermoforming)
  • 2. Stretching — viscoelastic deformation of the sheet as it is formed against the mold
  • 3. Cooling — conductive heat transfer from the hot formed part to the cold mold surface

⚠️ Heating is usually the rate-limiting step in continuous thermoforming. Choosing the correct lamp wavelength to match the plastic's absorption spectrum is critical to maximizing heating efficiency and throughput.

3. Radiative Heating — The Physics

The plastic sheet is heated by infrared (IR) radiation from lamps above (and sometimes below) the sheet. The key principle is: the lamp's emission spectrum must match the plastic's absorption spectrum for efficient heating.

Heating Time Formula

📐 theat = (ρ h cP ΔT) / (a Plamp)
where: h = sheet thickness [m], ρ = density [kg/m³], cP = specific heat [J/kg·K], a = absorption coefficient (dimensionless, 0–1), Plamp = lamp power density [W/m²], ΔT = required temperature rise [K]

Heating time scales linearly with sheet thickness (unlike injection molding cooling, which scales with thickness squared). Doubling the sheet thickness doubles the heating time.

Polymer Absorption Spectra

Polypropylene (PP) has high absorption between 3 and 4 µm. To heat PP efficiently, a lamp with peak emission in that range (e.g., medium wave or carbon) should be chosen. If the lamp emits at wavelengths the plastic transmits rather than absorbs, the radiation passes through the sheet and heats the mold instead.

4. The Thermoforming Process Window

The thermoforming process window defines the range of temperature and forming pressure (vacuum level) within which good parts can be made. It is plotted on a stress–strain diagram at different temperatures, with the vacuum-induced stress and the maximum allowable elongation as the boundaries.

How the Process Window Works

  • Too cold — the material's yield stress exceeds the vacuum stress → the sheet does not deform → incomplete forming
  • Too hot — the material is too fluid / weak → uncontrolled thinning and potential tears before the mold is fully filled
  • Too much stretch (beyond maximum design elongation) → tearing or catastrophic thinning at corners
  • Within the window → controlled, reproducible deformation

5. Areal Draw Ratio (ADR)

The Areal Draw Ratio (ADR) is the ratio of the surface area of the formed part to the original flat sheet area. It quantifies how much the sheet has been stretched.

📐 ADR = Surface area of formed part / Area of original flat sheet
A flat sheet has ADR = 1. Deeper, more complex shapes have higher ADR values. Exceeding the maximum ADR causes excessive thinning and tearing.

6. Design Rules for Thermoforming

  • Avoid sharp corners in the mold — use a corner radius of at least 2 × sheet thickness or greater to prevent excessive thinning and tearing at corners
  • Draft angles mandatory — minimum 0.25° to allow part release from the mold; deeper parts need more draft
  • No undercuts unless multi-part tooling (split molds) is used — undercuts prevent part removal
  • Areal draw ratio > 2 requires careful optimization — non-uniformity of wall thickness increases rapidly with ADR
  • Material: thermoplastics only — thermosets cannot be thermoformed (they do not soften reversibly)

⚠️ Key consequence of thermoforming geometry: Wall thickness is inherently non-uniform — flat regions retain near-original thickness; corners and deep features thin significantly. This is the fundamental limitation vs injection molding.

7. Other Polymer Processes — Context

Thermoforming is one branch of a much wider polymer processing family. The process route depends on the starting material form (pellets vs sheet vs liquid) and the polymer type (thermoplastic TP, thermoset TS, or elastomer E).

Blow Molding — Related Process

Blow molding produces hollow parts (bottles, containers) by first injection-molding a preform (parison), then inflating it with air inside a blow mold. Production rates can reach 7,500–30,000 bottles per hour using a multi-mold wheel system. It shares the physics of biaxial stretching of a thermoplastic with thermoforming.

8. What's New in Thermoforming?

  • Bio-derived and biodegradable plastics — replacing conventional polymers for sustainability (e.g., PLA-based cups and packaging)
  • Paper thermoforming — complex paper-based packaging formed using heat and pressure (Vegware lids, coffee cup lids)
  • Carbon fiber thermoforming — a growing research area for automotive body panels; high ADR with carbon fiber-reinforced thermoplastic sheets remains challenging

9. Quick Reference — The Big Four Comparison

AttributeInjection MoldingThermoforming
RateHighGreater (parts/hour, continuous)
QualityGoodLess (non-uniform thickness, lower tolerances)
CostLow at high volumeLess per part, especially at lower volume
FlexibilityLow (high tooling cost, hard to change)Greater (simpler/cheaper molds; fewer material restrictions)

✔ Advantages of Thermoforming

  • Very high production rates (continuous process)
  • Lower tooling cost than injection molding
  • Large parts feasible (fridge liners, vehicle dashboards)
  • Short lead times for new products

✗ Limitations

  • Non-uniform wall thickness
  • Less complex shapes than injection molding
  • Lower dimensional accuracy
  • Thermoplastics only
  • Significant trim scrap (skeleton waste)
